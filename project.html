Brainstorming

Sketches. Brainstorming

Before we met up, we brainstormed potential ideas to implement. The initial plan was to trawl through research papers on IEEE, specifically the IEEE Annual International Symposium Virtual Reality conference, and get inspired or mix together different ideas proposed by the various papers. But an idea stayed in my mind; the professor was not asking for a ‘useful’, or ‘revolutionary’, or even ‘easier’ selection technique. No, we was simply asking that we create one and then define our own adjective. So, I wanted to go against the grain and find a ‘useless but fun’ selection technique. What was an annoying way of selecting something? What would be fun, but also not something you’d actually use?

A game would be fun. Gamification was and is a big concept in video game development, why not incorrectly apply it here? Thus, I thought of the idea to fly a plane to the object. The user would spawn a plane, then fly it all the way to its destination. Once we met up for the official brainstorming session, we kept brainstorming more ideas.

Once we met up for the official brainstorming session, we kept brainstorming more ideas. The final shortlist was as follows:

  • Plane selection technique
    • As described above, you fly a plane to select an object
  • Camera selection technique
    • You take a picture of the scene, using a retro polaroid camera, and the object in the center is the object that is selected

      • Camera selection sketching 1

    • Alternatively, you take a picture of a scene, using the same retro polaroid camera, then circle or mark off the object you want to select.

      • Camera selection sketching 2

  • Cone casting:
    • A cone, with its top at the controller, spreading out in the direction the controller is pointing, acts as the preselection area. The controller is then used to navigate the different slices of the cone, where the user can then select their object.
    • This was the boring backup option, the ‘useful’ selection technique, in case the professor did not approve of the first two ideas.

Cone casting was ultimately discarded due to it not being fun. We opted to go with the other two techniques, because we thought they would be fun to implement and use.

Final plane and camera selection techniques.

We created a presentation introducing the two techniques, and, on the professor’s advice, decided to go with the plane selection technique.

Concept

Back to menu

What is this technique

The flying plane technique is a unique and novel selection technique, whereby instead of boringly selecting an object and having it selected, we fly a remote controlled (RC) plane to the object. To set its initial direction, we point in the direction we want it to go. In many respects, the start resembles raycasting. However, if one were to select the object as in raycasting, they would be surprised that a plane spawns in front of them, flying in that direction.

How To Control The Airplane

To have the airplane set off, the secondary hand trigger needs to be pressed. Then, when in flight, the user needs to be able to control the plane’s three axes of freedom: yaw, roll, and pitch. The right controller is used to control yaw and pitch of the airplane, with the B and A buttons used to control roll. To select an object, the plane has to enter its bounding box, where the user can then press the primary index trigger to select an object.

High level overview

How the airplane flies is the most important aspect to program correctly, so I handled that first. An important design limitation is that, although the airplane has three degrees of freedom, a joystick only has two: horizontal and vertical. It's possible to use the B and A buttons to simulate the third axis of freedom, but users are less likely to be familiar, notice, or associate these buttons with the third axis. Thus, we have to choose two axes to act as the main plane controller.

Since pitch is the only degree of freedom that corresponds to vertical movement, it gets the vertical joystick movement. That leaves either yaw or roll for horizontal movement. Although these two, in theory, enable different movements, for our purposes they largely fulfill the same action. In testing, yaw was more consistent and easier to control than roll, in addition to mapping well to how a car operates, which more users would have had experience with. Yaw was therefore mapped to horizontal joystick movement, with roll relegated to the B and A buttons.

Diagram showing pitch, roll, and yaw

Additionally, the user can speed up the airplane by holding down the joystick controller and activating a turbo.

The user has to be able to spawn the airplane. To do so, they have to point in the direction of the object and then click the secondary hand trigger. This will spawn the airplane, with its initial direction set to the direction of the right controller.

Finally, for the user to be able to select something, the airplane has to collide with the object. It's not difficult to do, as it's a built-in function in Unity. However, the airplane constantly slamming against objects made it difficult to navigate between different objects, especially those that were obscured behind other objects. As such, the airplane is not physically present and intentionally clips through other objects. This has the added benefit of allowing the user to more easily select objects, as they can wait for the airplane to be more fully inside the object before selecting it.

Modifying AirplaneController

The file ‘AirplaneController’ was created to be able to respond to user interactions and adjust its flight direction. This script was provided to us with the Unity asset, and we used it as a baseline for the airplane script, which had already coded for us various different functions.

The modifications made to the script included:

  1. Reassigning the controls from keyboard commands to VR controller inputs.
  2. Disabling the collision check that disables the controls and sends the airplane into freefall.

Participants

To find participants to test our technique, we asked our friends and close ones for help. In the end, we collected three participants:

  • Danny, 24 y.o., linguistics student, my roommate.
  • John, 21 y.o., IGD student.
  • Jenny, 24 y.o., IGD student.

Before testing, we explained how our selection technique was going to work and then started the testing.

Results

Based on the testing, our technique proved to be a very complicated task for the following reasons:

Sharp Navigation

In order to control the plane, users used Up, Down, Left, and Right buttons on the joystick. As expected, it took each of the participants some time to get used to the controllers. According to Danny’s comments, the plane’s movements were too sharp, which forced her to be very focused during the entire flying process, especially when turning.

Inverting Controllers

While trying to hit the target, Danny and John accidentally turned the plane upside down without noticing it. Since the navigation was plane-centered, when the user pushed the joystick’s button to the left, the plane turned to its left. However, when the plane was upside-down, the controls reversed: when participants pressed the right button, they saw the plane turn left. This created confusion. Since participants did not immediately understand the reason for this change, it seemed as if the right button sometimes turned the plane right, and five minutes later it turned it left. As Danny commented, it felt like gaslighting.

Losing the Plane

Our plane flies straight without stopping, which makes it important for participants to concentrate on when they turn and where. If they didn’t select a product in time or missed it, they had to turn their plane around and try again. During our testing, all three participants lost their plane on the shelf at least once. Since the plane could fly through products on the shelf, participants often lost sight of it. Jenny admitted that she started panicking because of it. The tactic participants used to find the plane again was usually chaotically clicking buttons and searching for it visually. Eventually, all participants managed to find the plane again. Jenny shared that she expected there to be a button to summon the plane back to her.

Small Plane Size

Sometimes the products that participants had to select were close to them, but in some cases, they were quite far. When flying far away, the plane became ridiculously small. John even mentioned its resemblance to a fly. For participants who did the testing without the ability to move, selecting an object at a distance was a challenge. However, John successfully managed to do it.

In summary, all these obstacles made the testing process long and complicated. After the first participant, who got tired of the test after selecting the first object, we adjusted our approach. From that point, we limited testing to selecting only one object, and if participants had extra time and wanted to continue, they could attempt to select a second object.

All three participants successfully selected the first object. Only one out of three managed to select the second as well.

Due to these results, it was impossible to compare our technique to raycasting because of the significant usability differences. It was clear that for the purpose of fast object selection, raycasting is far more effective.

However, our selection method can be considered an entertaining game or challenge. Based on the overall impressions of the participants, all of them mentioned positive feelings.

  • One-third of the participants laughed during testing.
  • One participant said he was highly motivated to select an object and felt very happy when he succeeded.
  • Participants praised the plane's design, calling it "cute".
  • They also noted that this selection technique was unusual and different from anything they had experienced before.

Future work

Suggested Improvements

Based on the collected feedback, here are the improvements that, in our opinion, could enhance the effectiveness and user experience of our selection technique:

  • Make the turns softer and more predictable.
  • Introduce a maximum plane deviation so that it always stays bottom-down.
  • Switch to a plane point of view when flying it.

Furthermore, this selection technique could serve as the foundation for a puzzle video game based on selecting objects. We are convinced that it has a lot of potential.
